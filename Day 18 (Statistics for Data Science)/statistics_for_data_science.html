Statistics For Data Science


Types of Statistics

  • Descriptive Statistics (Summarizing data)
    • Measures of Central Tendency (Mean, Median, Mode)
    • Measures of Dispersion (Variance, Standard Deviation, Range)
    • Distribution shape (Skewness, Kurtosis)
  • Inferential Statistics (Drawing conclusions)
    • Sampling methods
    • Hypothesis testing
    • Confidence intervals
    • Regression analysis
    • Predicting population properties from a sample
  • Population ($N$): The complete set of items or events under study.
  • Sample ($n$): A subset of the population selected for analysis.

Measures Of Central Tendency

Population Mean    Sample Mean

$$ \mu = \frac{\sum x}{N} $$
$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} $$

Grades = [30, 40, 50, 70, 50, 90]

Mean = (30 + 40 + 50 + 70 + 50 + 90) / 6 = 55


Median

The middle value when data is sorted.

Grades (Sorted): [40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 100, 120]

Mean (Average): 74.2

Median: 70 (Since $n=7$, the 4th term is the median)

Outlier Impact

Notice how outliers affect the Mean but not the Median.

Grades (with Outlier): [40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 100, 120, 1500]

Mean (Average): 252.5 (Heavily affected by 1500)

Median: (70 + 80) / 2 = 75


Mode

Mode: The value that appears most frequently in a dataset.

Grades: [40, 40, 40, 40, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90, 100]

Mode: 40


Measures of Dispersion (Spread)


Variance

Variance measures how far a set of numbers is spread out from their average value.

 Population Variance          Sample Variance

$$ \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x - \mu)^2}{N} $$
$$ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1} $$


Standard Deviation

Standard Deviation indicates typical deviation from the mean. Ideally, low SD means data is clustered around the mean.

It is simply the square root of the variance, bringing the unit back to the original data scale (e.g., from $cm^2$ to $cm$).

 Population Std. Dev.     Sample Std. Dev.

$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x - \mu)^2}{N}} $$
$$ s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1}} $$

Variables

A variable is any characteristic, number, or quantity that can be measured or counted.

1. Numerical (Quantitative) Variables

  • Continuous Variable (Measurable, infinite possibilities within a range)
    • Student's exam grade: (e.g., 85.5, 90.3)
    • Student's height: (e.g., 165.2 cm, 183.4 cm)
    • Teacher's Salary: (e.g., $100,354.50)
  • Discrete Variable (Countable, distinct values)
    • Number of students in a classroom: (e.g., 25, 30, 35)
    • Number of active classes: (e.g., 4, 5, 6)

2. Categorical (Qualitative) Variables

  • Nominal Variables (Labels with no natural order)
    • Student's major (Math, CS, Physics)
    • Gender (Male, Female, Other)
    • Colors (Red, Blue, Green)
  • Ordinal Variables (Labels with a logical/ranked order)
    • Education level (High School < Bachelor's < Master's < PhD)
    • Letter grades (A, B, C, D, F)
    • Customer satisfaction (Happy, Neutral, Unhappy)

Random Variables

A variable whose value is unknown or a function that assigns values to each of an experiment's outcomes.

  • Discrete Random Variable: X = Number of heads in 10 coin tosses.
  • Continuous Random Variable: X = Exact time it takes to run 100 meters.
